01

The Four Answers

The ordering law of the whole system. Every trust surface answers these, in this order, or it does not ship.

The law
  1. What happened? → severity mark · eyebrow · recency · title
  2. What does it mean? → body, ≤3 lines, in the user's terms
  3. What should I do? → exactly one primary CTA, or none
  4. What else can I do? → ghost actions, then destructive, then ⋯
Consequences that bind: a ghost or destructive action may never precede the primary. A surface that has no answer to (3) renders (1) + (2) + (4) and does not fabricate a primary action — an echo banner offers only Dismiss. Destructive is always terminal in the stack, and is never full-width: an irreversible action must not read as the obvious one.
Which fill does the primary take?
Primary answers…FillExample
…the severity condition itself--sev (locked)sheet: "Open settings", "Allow microphone"
…a constructive recovery choice--accent-fillrecovery card: "Combine clips", "Retry"

A severity fill means "this button resolves the red thing." Combining clips is not resolving a severity — it is the ordinary, hoped-for path. Painting it red would make a good outcome look like an alarm, violating the report-not-alarm law of §07.

TrustRow — the shared atom

What is unified is the ordering, not the markup: every surface answers 1→4 in this sequence. The recovery card realizes it literally (.trustrow); the sheet centers the same order under a 56dp mark; the banner elides (3)→a single Stop and has no (4); the strip and settings chip stop after (2)+(3). The container and the dismissibility stay per-host, exactly as WarningCenterContract.md §6 specifies. We unify the atom, not the behavior.

┌──────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ [mark]  A1 eyebrow · recency             │

Hierarchy comes from weight and opacity, never size. The type scale is 9.5–15px, exactly as the Library's is 10–14.

07

Recovery cards

Six kinds + the merge states. An interruption is a report, not an alarm. The severity of each kind is severityColorFor(kind) (RecoveryCard.kt:246-253) transcribed verbatim — a design freeze may not silently re-assign meaning.

Severity per kind — source of truth, not a design choice
KindSeverityPulseWhy the code says so
KILLED_BY_SYSTEMhardyesthe only kind that recurs until the user changes an OS setting; it is the one card carrying a vendor-guidance CTA (:101 → pulsing = isHardSeverity)
SCHEDULED_ENDadvisory—"neutral/informational, NOT an alarm" (:248, verbatim comment)
KILLED_FORCE_STOP · SAFETY_STOPPED · ERROR_STOPPED · USER_STOPPEDsoft—the user acted, the OS acted, or the app reported — nothing is destroyed and nothing recurs
Do not re-derive this mapping. It is transcribed verbatim from RecoveryCard.kt:246–253, not chosen here. The law it encodes: red is earned by a condition the user must act on outside Rova to prevent — so KILLED_BY_SYSTEM is hard and pulsing, and "something went wrong once" (ERROR_STOPPED) is a report and stays amber. Re-tinting any kind is a behavior change: it needs an ADR and a new HTML iteration, never a transcription-time decision.

Recovery merge — idle → in-flight → failed → retry

In-flight disables Merge and Keep, and leaves Discard live — the user is never trapped. This adaptive stack is shipped behavior, preserved verbatim.

The strip's denominator is survivingArtifacts.size — the clip count the card already prints one line above — and its numerator is progress × that. Nothing else exists: RecoveryCardState carries no expected-segment count, and inventing one needs a schema change (NO-GO #4). Today's chip prints N / N for every frame of the merge (RecoveryCard.kt:408 passes artifactCount twice), which is the artifact-count-as-progress proxy principle 1 forbids. The frozen strip prints filled / total, and the two numbers agree with the clip line. The strip is rendered only while merging; idle, it is a row of filled cells restating a number already on screen, so it is not drawn.

11

Responsive & reduced motion

Responsive
  • Rotation. Banner and chip rotate with the Record chrome group. The sheet stays bottom-anchored to the device, not the chrome.
  • Width. Sheet caps at 420dp content width and centers on wide screens/foldables. Body copy caps at 60ch.
  • Text scale. Every height is a min-height, never fixed — 200% font scale (SC 1.4.4) must not clip a label. CTA stacks reflow vertically; the severity chip wraps below the title rather than truncating it.
  • Truncation. Title 2 lines · body 3 lines · the "why" expander owns the remainder. A severity label never truncates.
  • Insets. Banner respects status-bar + display cutout; sheet respects the navigation bar. Over-media surfaces never occlude the record FAB or the HUD.
  • reduceTransparency. Over-media substrates render fully opaque. Currently unread by these surfaces; the freeze wires it.
Motion — the whole inventory
  • Severity pulse, on exactly two dots: the snooze chip when the collapsed warning is a HardBlockSheet id (WarningSnoozeChip.kt:63), and the recovery tag when the kind is KILLED_BY_SYSTEM (RecoveryCard.kt:101). Nothing else pulses — an advisory chip that pulses makes the pulse mean nothing, and then it is decoration that fails the bar this inventory sets: an animation ships only if it carries a state no static mark carries.
  • ProcessingGlyph during merge. Distinguishes "working" from "hung" on a long silent operation.
  • Sheet enter/exit + chip collapse/restore. --t-container in, --t-exit out, --ease-std.
The rule that makes the gate safe: no animation may be the sole carrier of meaning — stated per surface, because the surfaces differ:
· Recovery card: the pulse rides a dot that sits beside an uppercase severity tag and a status glyph. Kill the motion and the tag still reads INTERRUPTED.
· Snooze chip: the chip's information is the count ("1 warning"), and that is text. Severity there is decoration — the chip does not claim to tell you which warning it is, only that one exists and where to tap. Nothing is lost when the pulse stops, because the pulse never carried the count.
Removed: CountdownRing — animation-shaped chrome that neither animated nor counted. It may return only when backed by a real countdown.

Shape primitives are not spacing. The ladder governs padding, gaps and radii. APPX-B · The pinned container — one token, one alpha

The banner's sub-text carried no AA rationale, unlike the sheet body (0.55α, documented) and the recovery body (0.65α, documented). It looked like the SC 1.4.3 suspect. Computed, it is not the failure. The snooze chip is.

What the numbers actually say. The banner sub-text clears AA at 0.88 — by 0.004. The snooze chip (WarningSnoozeChip.kt:80 — Color.Black, not pinSurface) reads 3.61:1 and fails. So there is one genuine violation and one pass with a four-thousandths margin that nobody had ever computed. Neither margin is defensible in a frozen token. Both close on the same move: pinSurface #0B0D14 at 0.94 — the alpha the recovery card already used — for the banner and the chip. Two raw 0xFF0B0D14 literals and three divergent alphas (0.88 / 0.94 / 0.55, one of them a different color entirely) collapse into one token at one value.

The container fix alone is sufficient; the ink change is separate and is disclosed. At the frozen container the chip label passes at its shipped 0.78 ink (see row 3, 10.76:1). The freeze nevertheless raises it to mediaInk 0.94 — the same disclosed move as the banner title's 0.88→0.94 — so that the three pinned surfaces share one ink scale rather than four alphas. That is an aesthetic unification, not an AA repair, and APPX-F lists it as such. No reader of this appendix should be able to mistake one for the other.

The standing rule of this appendix. Contrast is proven by the inspector, never by eye and never by hand. Two things follow, and both are binding on anyone who edits this table. One: no ratio may be typed in — every number in the Ratio column is rendered live by §03's sweep from the ink, the alpha and the backing beside it. Two: a computed ratio over a fabricated input is still a wrong number, so every input cites the source line it was read from (e.g. the chip's 0.78 ink is WarningSnoozeChip.kt:106, Color.White.copy(alpha = 0.78f) — not the 0.94 the pinned scale uses elsewhere). A hand-computed sRGB transfer curve is exactly one division away from asserting that a passing surface fails; a typed-in ink is exactly one alpha away from asserting that a failing surface passes. Cite the line, let the page do the arithmetic.

No structural scrim primitive. An 88%-opaque capsule was never the Library's 0%-opaque-thumbnail problem, and a 94% one certainly is not. Generalizing CaptionScrim here would be machinery bought for a bug that one number fixes.

APPX-D · Hue as ink — one resolver, and what is deliberately exempt
  • No fixed derivation works. Rendering severity text as a constant color-mix(sev 62%, textHigh) clears 4.5:1 on the 11 dark palettes and reads 2.85:1 on Daylight; the raw-hue dot reads 1.36:1 there (gold on pale gold). A constant accent-text = color-mix(accent 72%, tHigh) reads 4.24:1 on Daylight's severity-tinted chips. Every one of those numbers is a real specimen on the one shipping light palette, and every one of them is invisible if the design is validated on Aurora alone. Any hue used as ink must be resolved against whatever it sits on. One resolver, not two — and no third fixed mix.
  • resolveInk(hue, backing, target, top, mix) — a pure resolver; the deepen half is the house's DialogActionColors.resolve primitive reused. Direction is chosen by the backing's luminance (DARK_BACKING = 0.18, a declared constant, not a feel):
    · dark backing → the committed lighten derivation: mix(hue, mix, top), where top is the host's composited high ink and mix is 0.62 for a severity label, 0.72 for accent-as-text, and 0 for a mark (which stays the raw locked hue). Zero visual delta on all 11 dark palettes. The dark branch returns without consulting target; that it clears the target is a fact the on-load sweep proves, not an invariant the function enforces.
    · light backing → deepen(hue, t) at the minimum t ∈ {0, .12, … .6} that clears the target. Hue is preserved on this branch only — deepen is a proportional RGB scale; the lighten branch mixes toward the host ink and therefore desaturates by design.
    Only Daylight ever takes the deepen branch. Targets: 4.5:1 label / accent text, 3:1 mark.
  • The locked hue is not the rendered hue — and that is the point. #EF4444 remains the single locked identity of "hard" across all 12 palettes; resolveInk is a rendering function over it, never a second token. This is the same separation as accent-vs-accent-fill. Compose: a pure ResolveInk.of(hue, backing, target, top, mix) + TokenContrastTest.
  • Themed dim/body ink is quietTextColor, not textDim. Daylight's textDim is #141A28 @ 0.60α: it reads 4.51:1 on bare white — and 4.25:1 on a severity-tinted strip, 4.37:1 inside a 7% ghost fill, 4.42:1 in the fail box. Every one of those is a real frozen specimen. The app already solved this: QuietText.kt:17 drops the alpha on a light scheme and returns the solid ink, because "alpha-dimming is mathematically below AA over a light background." Every themed --ink-dim / --ink-body routes through it. Zero delta on the 11 dark palettes.
  • The 8 mark/label sites and their real backings. A mark is resolved against what is actually behind it, which is not always the surface: the history strip's chip sits on color-mix(sev 8%, surface), not on surface; the banner's icon box and Stop pill sit on the 0.94 capsule composited over the live frame, so they are resolved per frame. The sweep walks the eight INK_SITES keys — sheeticon · pinchip · banner · snooze · chip · stripchip · set · recov, where banner resolves two backings (the 16% icon box and the 20% Stop pill) — × 4 severities × 12 palettes × 3 frames.
  • Explicitly exempt from SC 1.4.11 — and why. This spec does not claim "every border and surface clears 3:1." Four things never could, and each is a decoration whose meaning is carried elsewhere:
    · Severity chip fill (0.20α) — identity is carried by dot + uppercase label + glyph. The 0.14→0.20 bump (RecoveryCard.kt:300) bought perceptibility, not conformance.
    · Ghost / destructive CTA borders (12% ink, 30% hard) — 1.26–1.49:1 across the 12 palettes. The label is the control's identity; the border is a hairline hint. Removing it changes nothing a user can read.
    · Banner and chip severity borders (30% / 25%) — shipped chrome, decorative, same argument.
    · Progress cells (filled vs track — as low as 2.12:1 on Aurora) — the count they depict is printed as text adjacent to them ("2 / 4") and spoken by a polite live region. They are a redundant echo of a label, which is exactly the case SC 1.4.11 does not bind.
    Everything not on this list is asserted by the inspector and blocks the freeze if it fails.
  • One fixed mix survives, and it survives by proof. The destructive CTA's label — color-mix(sev-hard 62%, ink-high) — is not routed through the resolver. It sits on the untinted card surface, not on a 20% chip of its own hue, and the sweep asserts it on all 36 cells — the worst is 5.70:1 on Jade, the best 7.37:1 on Eclipse. It stays fixed because it is tested, not because it is special. Should a future host put it on a tinted backing, the assertion fails and the freeze breaks — which is the whole point of the sweep.
  • The dot is the 1.4.1 non-color-alone partner and carries ≥3:1 against its own backing — at every mark site, all of which have different backings. The on-load sweep proves all of them, in all 12 palettes, on all 3 frames.
  • The severity glow is a bloom, not a background blur. The sheet icon's radial bloom (sheetIconGlowBlur 22dp) and the recovery card's top-edge glow (recoveryCardGlowBlur 28dp) are shipped, decorative, contentDescription = null layers. They are not Modifier.blur on the record surface or on a banner background, and therefore do not touch NO-GO #3 or checkRecordSurfaceNoBlur. Stated here so a transcriber does not delete them as a gate violation.
